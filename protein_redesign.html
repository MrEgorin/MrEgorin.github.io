Protein Redesign

Our team, consisting of Yehor Franchuk, Vlad Dolganskyi, Maksym Zhuravel, Larysa Vynohradnyk, and German Lavrikov, under the supervision of Bohdana Hurieva and Iryna Poplevicheva, conducted native redesign of proteins containing the START domain.

This week, we investigated the molecular evolution of the START domain by constructing a phylogenetic tree.

We then used this analysis to semi-rationally redesign the domain across different organisms to enhance its properties.

Based on the phylogenetic analysis, five representative protein structures were selected for a semi-rational protein design.

The START domain is a conserved protein module critical for lipid binding and transport in various organisms. It plays a key role in cellular signaling and metabolism, particularly in sterol and lipid transfer processes.

Multiple sequence alignment revealed an RMSD score of 0.154, indicating high structural conservation.

RMSD score = 0.154

This represents an important step towards drug discovery by leveraging redesign informed by natural diversity. Exploring protein fold space may potentially offer novel candidates with greater efficacy, which would allow targeted drug delivery of lipophilic compounds.

In addition, synthetic proteins generated via this approach can be also used in various fields, including biosensing, and environmental sampling.

Native redesign was performed by excising the START domain from PDB files of well-characterized proteins, specifically CERT1 (Homo sapiens), using an automated script in Open Source PyMOL. Watch the process here.

Using the online tool P2Rank, which employs the HHMER approach, binding sites with high scores were identified. In PyMOL, conserved regions were highlighted and protected during the application of RFdiffusion, which generated a hydrophilic scaffold around the conserved lipid-binding core, facilitating cloning in prokaryotes.

The resulting structure lacked a biologically justified amino acid sequence, exhibiting poly-glycine stretches. ProteinMPNN was used to convert the PDB file's 3D structure into a 2D graph and subsequently into a FASTA sequence file.

The FASTA file was processed in AlphaFold 2, which leverages structural and evolutionary components to generate protein structures. The best structure among three variants was selected based on a pTM score of 0.9 (higher is better), confirming high solubility and stability.

Docking of the modified CERT1 protein (post-native redesign) with 2-[4-[3-tert-butyl-5-(2-pyridin-2-ylethyl)phenyl]phenyl]sulfonylethanol (9N3, compound B5) was performed using CB-Dock2, compared to the native protein-compound B5 structure.

The modified protein exhibited a higher number of amino acids involved in ligand binding, with a distinct pocket on the 3D protein surface, confirming its ability to adapt its conformation to the ligand.

To assess protein stability, molecular dynamics simulations were conducted for 0.2 nanoseconds under human physiological conditions using OpenMM.

Additional analyses confirmed the protein's scoring and stability metrics.
